## 1. Executive Summary

**Recommendation: B — Test Angi only with strict limits.** *(Confidence: 78%)*

The pay-per-lead offer ($750 cash for $1,000 in lead credit, first-year annual fee waived, leads can be paused) is a **structurally acceptable way to buy a controlled test** *if* the written terms match the verbal pitch. The maximum cash at risk ($750) is small relative to the information value of running a real 30-day test in Genesis's actual market. That is the core reason to test rather than refuse.

However, the recommendation is conditional, not enthusiastic, for three reasons:

2. **Angi's model is shared leads.** The same homeowner is typically sold to multiple pros. This structurally suppresses close rates and rewards speed-to-contact, not craftsmanship — which is a poor fit for a premium-positioned brand. *(Confidence: 85%)*
3. **The category mix matters more than the channel.** Handyman and small-repair leads on lead platforms skew toward price shoppers and tiny jobs. Genesis's profitability on Angi depends almost entirely on disciplined category selection, fast response, and converting small calls into larger scoped work. *(Confidence: 80%)*

**Why not A (Do not use Angi):** Refusing outright forfeits cheap, real-world data about lead quality in Genesis's exact zips. The downside is capped and known; the upside (a working acquisition channel during a growth phase) is material. Refusing is only correct if the written terms are bad.

**Why not C (Proceed aggressively):** There is zero proof of lead quality or ROI yet. Aggressive spend or a 12-month contract before a test would convert a capped, known risk into an uncapped, unknown one. *(Confidence: 90%)*

### What is still missing (must be resolved before paying)

**Bottom line:** Test small, in writing, with a hard cap, in a narrow set of high-value zips and categories. Do **not** sign the 12-month contract until the short test proves a profitable close rate and average ticket.

## 3. Break-Even Math

**Modeling base case:** $47 average lead cost → **~21 leads** from the $1,000 credit. *(SALES CLAIM for price; VERIFIED arithmetic for counts.)*

**Definitions (to avoid the most common contractor error):**
- **Revenue / gross invoice** = what the customer pays.
- **Net profit** = revenue − materials − labor (helper cost + owner time) − overhead allocation. **Break-even uses NET, never gross.**

### 3a. Booked jobs by close rate (21 leads)

| Close rate | Booked jobs (21 × rate) |
|---|---|
| 5% | 1.05 ≈ **1** |
| 10% | 2.10 ≈ **2** |
| 15% | 3.15 ≈ **3** |
| 20% | 4.20 ≈ **4** |
| 25% | 5.25 ≈ **5** |

*(VERIFIED arithmetic.)*

### 3b. Required average NET profit per booked job to recover the $750 cash

Formula: **$750 ÷ (21 leads × close rate)**

| Close rate | Booked jobs | Required NET profit / job to break even |
|---|---|---|
| 5% | 1.05 | **$714** |
| 10% | 2.10 | **$357** |
| 15% | 3.15 | **$238** |
| 20% | 4.20 | **$179** |
| 25% | 5.25 | **$143** |

*(VERIFIED arithmetic.)*

### 3c. Translating net profit back into invoice size (sanity check)

Assume a blended **net margin of ~35%** on small service/repair work after materials, helper labor, drive time, and overhead. *(ASSUMPTION, confidence 55% — Genesis should replace with its own margin.)* Required **gross invoice** ≈ required net ÷ 0.35:

| Close rate | Required NET / job | Implied gross invoice / job @35% margin |
|---|---|---|
| 5% | $714 | ~$2,040 |
| 10% | $357 | ~$1,020 |
| 15% | $238 | ~$680 |
| 20% | $179 | ~$510 |
| 25% | $143 | ~$410 |

*(VERIFIED arithmetic on a 35% ASSUMPTION.)*

### 3d. Reading the table

- **At a 5% close rate, the test is very hard to justify** — it needs a single ~$2,000 job to break even, on only ~1 expected booking. High variance; one missed lead = loss. *(Confidence 80%.)*
- **The realistic profitable zone is 15–25% close**, which requires average invoices of roughly **$400–$680** — entirely achievable for electrical/multi-item handyman work, *not* achievable for single $89 fixture swaps. *(Confidence 75%.)*
- **Conclusion:** The test breaks even comfortably *only if* Genesis (a) closes ≥15% of leads and (b) keeps average tickets above ~$450 net-equivalent. Both are operational disciplines, not platform features. *(RECOMMENDATION, confidence 78%.)*

> **Important nuance:** Break-even on $750 is the *floor*. To call the channel a *success* worth continuing, Genesis should target **2–3× return on lead spend** (i.e., recover $1,500–$2,250 in net profit), not merely break even. A channel that only breaks even is a waste of operational attention.

## 4. Lead Quality Risk

This is the section most likely to determine real-world ROI, and the area where sales claims and reality diverge most.

### 4a. Shared-lead mechanics *(Confidence 85%)*
Angi's pay-per-lead model generally sells the **same homeowner contact to multiple pros**. Implications:
- The customer may receive 3–5+ calls within minutes. The job is effectively a **race**, not a referral.
- Close rates are structurally depressed vs. exclusive leads. Industry experience for shared home-service leads commonly lands in the **10–20% close** range for fast responders, lower for slow ones. *(ASSUMPTION, confidence 60%.)*
- **This is the single biggest unknown.** "How many pros are charged for this same lead?" must be answered in writing.

### 4b. Speed-to-contact *(Confidence 90%)*
- Contacting within **~1–5 minutes** dramatically outperforms contact after 30+ minutes. On shared leads, the first pro to reach a live human often wins the appointment before competitors connect.
- Practical consequence: **if Genesis cannot commit to sub-5-minute response during business hours, the expected close rate drops toward the bottom of the range and the test is likely to fail.** The response system in §10 is not optional — it is the deciding factor.

### 4c. The "leakage" categories that destroy ROI

| Leak type | Effect on ROI | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| **Unanswered calls** | Pay for a lead, never reach a human. | Immediate text + call cadence (§10); log no-contact for credit claims. |
| **Tire kickers / "just curious"** | Burns response time, never books. | Fast phone qualification; don't drive out without scope + photos. |
| **Price shoppers** | Collecting 3–5 bids; race to the bottom. | Premium positioning, scheduled paid service-call model; decline lowball jobs. |
| **Duplicate leads** | Charged twice for one customer. | Track by phone number; dispute duplicates for credit. |
| **Wrong-category leads** | Roofing/HVAC/etc. sent to a handyman. | Tight category selection; dispute for credit. |
| **Already-hired customers** | Customer booked someone before you called. | Speed-to-contact; dispute "already hired" for credit. |

*(Confidence 80% on mechanism; financial magnitude depends on Angi's actual lead quality, which is unverified.)*

### 4d. Must be confirmed **in writing** before signup
1. Exact number of pros sold/charged per lead. **(Critical.)**
2. The written **credit/refund policy**: which of the leak types above qualify for credit, how disputes are filed, and time limits.
3. Whether credits are **account-only or cash-refundable**.
4. Whether Genesis can **manually approve leads before being charged** (changes the entire risk profile).
5. Hard cap availability and true no-penalty pause.

> **Confidence that lead quality will be "good" as pitched: 45%.** Not because Angi is bad, but because shared home-service leads are inherently noisy and the rep has not quantified the noise. The credit/dispute policy is the real insurance — get it in writing.

## 7. Categories to Avoid or Be Careful With

*(RECOMMENDATION, confidence 75%.)*

### Avoid at first
- **TV mounting:** Heavily price-shopped, low ticket ($80–$150), commoditized by big-box/handyman apps. A $47 lead for a $100 job with a 15% close rate loses money unless bundled into a larger visit.
- **Minor appliance repair:** Often needs brand-specific parts/specialists; high callback risk; mismatched customer expectations.
- **Any single "swap one fixture" micro-job** as a standalone: a $89 outlet swap cannot absorb a $47 lead cost at any realistic close rate. **These are only worth it when bundled** into a minimum service-call structure (see below).

### The "tiny job" math problem
A standalone job under **~$250 gross** generally cannot justify a $47 lead at sub-25% close rates (see §3c). **Mitigation:** enforce a **minimum service-call charge** (e.g., $X for the first hour/visit) and use the visit to scope additional work. Convert "one outlet" into "let me check the rest of the panel/room."

### Georgia licensing / compliance caution *(Confidence 75%; not legal advice)*
- **Electrical work in Georgia requires a state electrical contractor license.** Genesis must restrict electrical category leads to work it is **licensed and comfortable** performing, and ensure marketing/category language does not imply unlicensed electrical contracting.
- Use **careful category language**: market "handyman / home repair / fixture installation" for general work, and only advertise electrical contracting within the licensed scope.
- For **commercial** work, confirm insurance limits and any permitting/licensing differences before accepting commercial categories.
- **Action:** Confirm with Angi that **electrical categories can be limited** to Genesis's licensed scope (see §13), and have the owner verify current GA license coverage against each electrical sub-category.

## 9. Service Area Strategy

**Recommendation: Start NARROW and near base, with a small intown premium pilot — i.e., a controlled split, weighted to the northwest.** *(Confidence 75%.)*

Reasoning:
- A wide radius on a $750 test **dilutes data** and **inflates drive time**, destroying net profit per job. With only ~21 leads, spreading them across 22 zips yields ~1 lead per zip — statistically useless.
- **Drive time is a direct cost.** An intown job 45–60 minutes from Acworth with paid parking can erase the margin advantage of the higher ticket. The northwest zips (30062/30080/30060/30101) deliver **strong tickets with short drives** — the best profit-per-hour.
- **Helper logistics:** Tight clusters let helpers run multiple jobs/day. Scattered intown jobs strand crews in traffic.

**Recommended Phase-1 geography (for the test):**
- **Primary (≈70% of leads): NW cluster** — 30062, 30080, 30060, 30101, 30126. Short drives, good income, easy parking, helper-efficient.
- **Premium pilot (≈30% of leads): tight intown cluster** — 30306, 30307, 30327, 30305. Test whether premium ticket + brand fit overcomes drive/parking cost. **Measure profit-per-hour, not just profit-per-job.**

Do **not** open all 22 zips at once. Expand only after the test reveals which cluster produces the best **net profit per helper-hour**.

## 10. Response Speed System (1–5 minute response)

The entire ROI depends on this. Goal: **a human contact attempt within 5 minutes**, every time, during business hours. *(RECOMMENDATION; mechanism confidence 85%.)*

### 10a. Trigger & ownership
- Route Angi lead notifications to **phone push + a dedicated channel** (the owner or a designated "lead responder").
- **One person owns response during business hours.** Define after-hours rule (auto-text immediately, call next morning by a set time).

### 10b. Initial call script (within 5 minutes)
> "Hi [Name], this is [Name] with **Genesis Executive Home Services** — you just requested help with **[category]** on Angi. I can help with that. Do you have **two minutes** so I can understand the job and see if we're a good fit? … Great — tell me what's going on. … Got it. To give you an accurate price and the right tech, can you **text me a couple of photos** at this number? I can usually get you on the schedule **[today/this week]**."

Key moves: name the brand, confirm the category, qualify fast, **ask for photos**, propose a **scheduled paid service call** (not a free estimate trip).

### 10c. If no answer — text follow-up #1 (immediately after the missed call)
> "Hi [Name], this is [Name] with **Genesis Executive Home Services** about your **[category]** request. I can help and have availability **[this week]**. What's the best time for a quick 2-min call? You can also text me photos of the issue and your **zip code** and I'll get you a price."

### 10d. Follow-up #2 (if still no response, ~1–2 hours later, same day)
> "Following up on your **[category]** project — still happy to help. If you send a photo and your address/zip, I can confirm pricing and a time. If you've already booked someone, just reply 'all set' and I'll close it out. — [Name], Genesis"

(That last line surfaces **already-hired** leads quickly so they can be logged for a **credit dispute**.)

### 10e. Fast phone qualification (decide in <2 minutes)
Ask, in order:
1. **What** exactly needs doing? (scope + category match)
2. **Where** — zip/neighborhood? (in service area?)
3. **When** — urgency/timeline?
4. **Photos** — can you text them now?
5. **Decision/ownership** — own or rent? (rentals → PM upsell path)

### 10f. Avoid wasting time on bad leads
- **No free drive-out estimates** for small jobs — book a **paid service call** or quote from photos.
- Disqualify politely if: out of area, wrong category, expects free quote on a tiny job, or clearly price-shopping for the lowest bid.
- **Immediately log** wrong-category / duplicate / already-hired leads for **credit disputes** (see §11).

### 10g. Converting small jobs into scheduled paid work
- Anchor on a **minimum service-call rate** that covers the first hour + a slice of the lead cost.
- "While I'm there, I can also look at [related items] — most homes in [neighborhood] have a few small things." → turns a 1-item call into a multi-item invoice (the §3 average-ticket lever).
- For rentals/PM: "We do **turnover punch lists** and ongoing maintenance — want me to send our property-management info?"

### 10h. Tracking
Every lead is logged within the response window in the §11 sheet/CRM, including **minutes-to-contact** (the metric that predicts close rate).
