# Prehung Interior Door Installation and Trim Guide

**The job:** install the new prehung door (a door that comes from the store already attached by its hinges inside its own wooden frame) in the doorway between the finished room and the unfinished workshop, then trim it out and connect the baseboard.

**Written for:** a first-time installer with no carpentry experience. Every step is one action. Do the steps in order and do not skip ahead.

**Time to allow:** four to six hours of working time, plus drying time for glue, filler, caulk, and paint.

---

## About This Specific Doorway — Read This First

This guide was written from photographs of the actual work area. Here is what is true on this job:

- **The door** is a white six-panel door (a door with six raised rectangle shapes pressed into each face). It is hollow core (built like a light sandwich — two thin skins over a mostly empty inside — so it is light but easy to dent). It is still strapped shut in its factory packaging and is leaning against the dark navy wall. The round hole for the door knob is already drilled, but **no knob comes with it** — the knob is a separate purchase.
- **The finished room side** of the wall has drywall (the smooth board material that covers wall framing) that is already installed and painted — gray walls plus one dark navy wall.
- **The workshop side** of the same wall has **no drywall at all** — just bare wall studs (the upright wooden framing boards inside a wall) with insulation between them. This matters a great deal in Phase 6, because trim cannot be installed on a wall that does not exist yet.
- **The floor** in the finished room is brand new luxury vinyl plank (wood-look plastic flooring planks). It is a floating floor (a floor that is not glued or nailed down — the planks lock to each other and simply rest on the surface below, so the whole floor must stay free to expand and shift slightly). The workshop floor is bare subfloor (the rough structural floor surface under a finished floor) and sits slightly lower.
- **The baseboard** (the trim board that runs along the bottom of the wall where it meets the floor) is not installed yet. The primed white boards for it are already on site, leaning against the walls. **The door casing gets installed first, then the baseboard runs up to it.** Phase 7 explains why.
- **Electrical warning:** there is at least one open electrical box (a plastic or metal cup set into the wall that holds wiring connections) with exposed wires and **no cover plate** in the gray wall beside the opening, at about light-switch height. There is also loose wiring visible in the open workshop walls. Phase 1 deals with this before anything else happens.

## Tools Needed

- **Tape measure**
- **Four-foot level** (a long straight tool with bubble windows that shows when something is perfectly vertical or perfectly horizontal)
- **Two-foot level or torpedo level** (a shorter version of the same tool, for spots where the long one does not fit, such as the top of the door frame)
- **Hammer**
- **Nail set** (a small steel punch held against a nail head and struck with a hammer, used to sink the nail slightly below the wood surface without leaving hammer dents)
- **Utility knife** (a razor knife with replaceable blades)
- **Miter saw** (a power saw on a pivoting base that makes exact straight and angled cuts, sometimes called a chop saw) — or a **miter box** (a plastic guide with pre-cut angle slots) and a **fine-tooth handsaw**
- **Combination square** (an adjustable square with a sliding ruler, used here to mark a small, even setback line for the casing)
- **Pencil**
- **Stud finder** (a small electronic tool that beeps or lights up when it passes over a wooden stud hidden behind drywall)
- **Small flat pry bar** (a thin flat steel bar for gently levering things apart or pulling nails)
- **Drill that can also drive screws**, with a cross-shaped screwdriver tip (called a Phillips tip) and a set of small drill bits
- **Sharp wood chisel, about 1 inch wide** (a beveled steel blade struck by hand or hammer to shave small amounts of wood)
- **Non-contact voltage tester** (a pen-shaped tester that lights up or beeps near a live electrical wire without touching it)
- **Caulk gun** (a metal frame tool that squeezes caulk out of its tube)
- **Putty knife** (a small flat blade for spreading filler)
- **Safety glasses**
- **Brad nailer and finish nailer if available** (nail guns that shoot thin trim nails; a brad nailer shoots the thinnest ones, called brads). If you do not have nail guns, a hammer and the nail set do the same job, just slower — the guide notes the difference where it matters.
- **Two sawhorses** (folding stands that hold a board at waist height for cutting) — set them up in the workshop
- **Vacuum or broom**
- **Clean rags** and a damp sponge
- **A second person** — strongly recommended. The door unit is not heavy, but it is large, floppy, and awkward for one person.

## Materials Needed

- **The prehung door unit** (already on site, leaning against the dark navy wall)
- **One door knob set** — either a passage knob (a knob with no lock, used for rooms that do not need privacy) or a privacy knob (a knob with a simple push-button lock). The set includes the latch bolt (the spring-loaded angled tongue that pokes out of the door edge) and the strike plate (the small metal plate screwed to the door frame that the latch clicks into).
- **One bundle of wood shims** (thin tapered pieces of wood used to fill gaps and adjust alignment)
- **Finish nails, 2-1/2 inches long** (thin nails with very small heads, made for trim work) — these fasten the door frame to the wall framing
- **Finish nails or brad nails, 2 inches long** — these fasten the outer edge of casing and the baseboard to the wall framing
- **Brad nails, 1-1/4 inches long** (the thinnest trim nails) — these fasten the inner edge of the casing to the door frame
- **One wood screw, 3 inches long** — goes behind the top hinge to carry the door's weight long-term
- **Door casing** (the trim boards that frame around the door opening) — three pieces at least 7 feet long cover one side of the doorway. Buy enough for both sides (six pieces), but expect to install only the finished-room side today. Phase 6 explains why.
- **The baseboard boards** already on site
- **Wood glue**
- **Wood filler or lightweight spackling** (a soft paste that dries hard, used to fill nail holes)
- **Paintable caulk** (a flexible paste squeezed into gaps to seal them — buy the kind labeled both "acrylic latex" and "paintable")
- **One fine sanding sponge** (a sponge block coated in very fine sanding grit)
- **Trim paint** (white, in a semi-gloss or satin finish) and a small brush
- **Wall touch-up paint** in the room's wall colors (the gray and the dark navy)
- **Painter's tape** (low-stick masking tape that peels off without damage)
- **One blank cover plate** (a flat plastic plate that screws over an electrical box to close it safely) — or have the planned switch or outlet installed by a qualified person before the job is called done
- **Floor protection:** the drop cloth already in the room, plus the flattened cardboard saved from the door's packaging

## Phase 1 — Make the Area Safe and Protect the New Floor

1. Read this entire guide once, start to finish, before touching anything. The order of the work is most of the skill.
2. Put your safety glasses where you will actually wear them — on your face for every step that involves cutting, nailing, drilling, or chiseling.
3. Move the tall white shelf unit at least 4 feet away from the doorway opening.
4. Move the storage bin and anything else sitting within 4 feet of the opening on either side.
5. Walk both sides of the opening slowly and find **every** open electrical box and loose wire near the doorway. There is at least one open box with wires in the gray wall beside the opening at about light-switch height — there may be more, including low on the wall and in the open workshop framing.
6. At the electrical panel (the gray metal box of switches where power enters the home), turn off the breaker (the switch in that panel) that controls those wires. If the panel labels are missing or unclear, turn off the breakers for this whole area of the home while you work.

    **Common Mistake:** trusting the breaker label without testing. Panel labels are wrong in many homes. If you skip the test in the next step and the label was wrong, driving a nail near a live wire — or brushing one with your hand — can burn or kill you.

7. Hold the non-contact voltage tester against each wire in each open box and confirm it stays silent and unlit. Only then treat the wires as dead.
8. Measure from the edge of the doorway opening to the nearest edge of the open electrical box, and write the number down. Casing is about 3-1/2 inches wide, and you will need this number in Phase 6.
9. If that measurement is less than 4 inches, stop and talk to the person in charge before going any further — an electrician may need to move the box so the trim can fit. Do not plan to cover any part of an electrical box with trim, ever.
10. Spread the drop cloth over the floor in front of the opening, covering the area where you will be standing and working.
11. Set up your saw on the sawhorses **in the workshop**. All cutting happens on the workshop side — never over the new floor.
12. Stage your tools and materials on the drop cloth or in the workshop, not loose on the bare planks.
13. Sweep or vacuum the floor inside the doorway opening where the door frame will sit, so the frame rests on clean flooring.

> **Check Your Work — end of Phase 1.**
> **Look at:** the tester results, the floor, and the work area.
> **Correct looks like:** every wire near the opening tested dead; the shelf and bin are clear of the work zone; the drop cloth covers the floor where you will work; the saw is set up in the workshop; the distance from the opening to the electrical box is written down and is at least 4 inches.

## Phase 2 — Unpack and Inspect the Door

14. With your helper, lay the door unit flat on the drop cloth, packaging still on.
15. Cut the plastic banding straps with the utility knife, always cutting away from your body.

    **Common Mistake:** cutting toward yourself or holding the strap under tension with your free hand. The straps are stretched tight and snap loose suddenly when cut — keep your face and free hand clear.

16. Peel off the cardboard corner protectors and any plastic wrap, and set the cardboard aside flat — you will reuse it as floor protection.
17. Find the shipping retainer that holds the door shut inside its frame. It is usually one of these: a plastic plug pushed through the round knob hole, a plastic bracket, or one or two small nails driven through the frame into the door edge. **Leave it in place for now.**

    **Common Mistake:** pulling the retainer out as part of unpacking. Without it, the door swings open freely while you carry the unit, which twists the hinges, smashes fingers, and dents the door and the new floor.

18. Inspect both faces of the door slab (the door panel itself, separate from its frame) for dents, cracks, or scuffs. Lift one edge with your helper to see the underside — do not drag it.
19. Inspect the frame: the head jamb (the top horizontal board of the door frame) should be tightly joined to the two side jambs (the upright boards of the frame), with no splits at the corners.
20. Check every hinge screw with the cross-shaped screwdriver tip and snug up any that turn.
21. Measure the unit's overall outside width — from the outside face of one side jamb to the outside face of the other — and write it down.
22. Measure the unit's overall outside height — from the bottom ends of the jambs to the top of the head jamb — and write it down.
23. Measure the jamb depth — the distance across the flat of the jamb board from its finished-room edge to its workshop edge. It is usually 4-9/16 inches. Write it down; Phase 3 uses it.
24. Stand the unit up with your helper and hold it near the opening in the position it will be installed, with the door set to swing **into the finished room**. On this job the door must swing into the finished room because the workshop side has a lower, unfinished floor and no finished wall for the door to sit against.
25. Note which side the hinges land on and which side the knob hole lands on when the unit is held that way. The knob side should be the same side as the open electrical box, since that box is likely the future light switch. A prehung door's hinge side cannot be changed, so if the knob lands on the opposite side, tell the person in charge before installing — do not just continue.
26. If you found any damage in steps 18 through 20, stop, photograph it, and report it before installing — a damaged unit can usually be exchanged, but not after it is nailed into a wall.

> **Check Your Work — end of Phase 2.**
> **Look at:** the slab faces, the frame corners, the hinges, your notepad, and the retainer.
> **Correct looks like:** no dents or splits anywhere; hinge screws snug; three measurements written down (unit width, unit height, jamb depth); the shipping retainer still holding the door shut; you know the door swings into the finished room and you know which side the knob lands on; the packaging cardboard is saved flat.

## Phase 3 — Check the Rough Opening

The rough opening is the framed hole in the wall, built from wall studs, that the door unit fits into. The upright stud on each side of the opening is called a jack stud (the framing board the door frame gets fastened to), and the horizontal framing board across the top is called the header.

27. Measure the width of the opening — jack stud face to jack stud face — near the bottom. Write it down.
28. Measure the width at the middle. Write it down.
29. Measure the width at the top. Write it down, and circle the smallest of the three numbers.
30. Subtract the unit's outside width (step 21) from that smallest opening width. The answer should be between 1/2 inch and 1-1/4 inches. That leftover space is the shim gap. If the answer is smaller than 1/4 inch or bigger than 2 inches, stop and report it — the opening needs framing work that is beyond this guide.
31. Measure the height of the opening from the finished floor to the underside of the header, on the left side. Write it down.
32. Measure the same height on the right side. Write it down.
33. Subtract the unit's outside height (step 22) from the smaller height number. The answer should be at least 3/8 inch. If the unit is taller than the opening, stop and report it.
34. Lay the four-foot level flat on the floor across the opening, spanning the two spots where the frame legs will sit, and read the bubble.
35. If the bubble is off center, lift the low end of the level until the bubble centers, then measure the gap under the lifted end. Write down that gap size and **which side of the opening sits higher**. You will use this in Phase 4.

    **Common Mistake:** skipping the floor check because the floor is new and "looks flat." If the frame goes in with one leg on a high spot, the whole frame tilts: the gap above the door becomes wedge-shaped, the door rubs, and the latch misses its plate. Thirty seconds with a level now prevents an hour of tearing trim off later.

36. Check the hinge-side of the opening for plumb (perfectly vertical, straight up and down) in the in-and-out direction: hold the four-foot level vertically, flat against the inside face of the jack stud, and read the bubble. Note how far and which way it leans (toward the room or toward the workshop).
37. Check the same side for plumb in the left-and-right direction: hold the level vertically against the painted wall surface right beside the opening edge, and read the bubble. Note the lean.
38. Repeat both plumb checks on the latch side of the opening and note the results.
39. If any face leans more than 1/4 inch over the height of the level, plan to correct for it with shims in Phase 4. If anything leans more than 1/2 inch, stop and report it before installing.
40. Measure the opening diagonally from the top-left corner to the bottom-right corner. Write it down.
41. Measure the other diagonal, top-right to bottom-left. If the two diagonals are within 1/4 inch of each other, the opening is square (its corners are true right angles) — close enough to proceed.
42. Measure the wall's total thickness at the opening: the depth of the stud plus the drywall on the room side. On this wall it will be about 4 inches, because there is drywall on only one side.
43. Compare that wall thickness to the jamb depth you wrote down in step 23 (about 4-9/16 inches). The jamb is deeper than this wall on purpose — it was sized for a wall with drywall on **both** sides. Since the workshop side has no drywall yet, the extra depth (about 1/2 inch) must all stick out past the studs on the **workshop side**, and the jamb edge must sit exactly flush (perfectly even with the surface, not sticking out or sunk in) with the painted drywall on the **room side**.

    **Common Mistake:** centering the frame in the wall to "split the difference." If you do that here, the jamb sits behind the painted drywall on the room side, the casing can never sit flat, and the finished side — the side everyone sees — looks wrong. On this job the rule is simple: **flush to the painted room side, all the extra hangs into the workshop.**

44. Run your hand and eyes over the inside faces of the opening and pull out any old nails, screws, or staples with the hammer or pry bar.
45. Hold the two-foot level against the underside of the header to check for sag. A dip of more than about 1/4 inch is worth reporting before you continue.

> **Check Your Work — end of Phase 3.**
> **Look at:** your written list of numbers.
> **Correct looks like:** you have written down — opening width at three heights, opening height at both sides, the floor's high side and how much higher it is, the plumb results for both sides, both diagonals, and the wall thickness. The unit fits the opening with 1/2 inch to 1-1/4 inches of side room and at least 3/8 inch of height room. You can say out loud which side of the floor is higher and why the jamb must sit flush to the room side.

## Phase 4 — Set the Door Frame: Shim, Plumb, and Fasten

How the fastening works in this phase: you will tack nails (drive them only partway in, so they hold position but can still be pulled out) while you check everything, and only drive them fully home once the door proves it swings correctly. Shims always go in as **pairs** — two shims pushed in from opposite directions so their tapers overlap and form a flat, solid block.

46. If the floor gap you wrote down in step 35 was more than 1/8 inch: measure that amount up from the bottom of the frame leg that will sit on the floor's **high** side, and draw a square pencil line across the leg.
47. Cut the leg off at that line with the saw. Shortening the high-side leg is what lets the top of the frame sit level. If the floor was level within 1/8 inch, skip steps 46 and 47.

    **Common Mistake:** trimming the wrong leg. Stop and say it out loud before cutting: the floor's high side gets the shorter leg. Cutting the low-side leg doubles the tilt, and legs cannot be uncut.

48. With your helper, carry the unit to the opening, door still retained shut, and set the bottoms of the legs on the floor in the opening.
49. Tip the unit up into the opening so the door will swing into the finished room.
50. Slide the unit left or right until the side gaps are roughly equal.
51. Adjust the unit front to back until both jamb edges sit exactly flush with the painted drywall face, checked by feel and by eye at the top, middle, and bottom of both sides. Your helper holds the unit there.
52. On the hinge side, at the height of the **top hinge**, slide a pair of shims into the gap between the jamb and the jack stud until they are snug — filling the gap firmly without pushing the jamb over.

    **Common Mistake:** using a single shim instead of an opposing pair. One shim is a wedge — it tilts the jamb as you tighten it, and every nail you drive afterward locks in that tilt. Always overlap two, points in opposite directions, so the jamb stays flat.

53. Drive one 2-1/2 inch finish nail through the jamb, through the shim pair, into the jack stud — but leave about 1/2 inch of the nail sticking out so it can still be pulled. That is a tack.
54. Hold the four-foot level vertically, flat against the wide face of the hinge jamb, and check plumb in the in-and-out direction. Move the bottom of the unit in or out until the bubble centers. If plumb and flush-with-drywall disagree by more than about 1/8 inch, favor plumb on the hinge side and tell the person in charge — a leaning hinge jamb makes the door swing on its own.
55. At the height of the **bottom hinge**, slide in a shim pair until snug.
56. Hold the level against the narrow edge of the hinge jamb (the edge facing the finished room) and check plumb in the left-and-right direction. Push the bottom shim pair deeper, or ease it out, until the bubble centers.
57. Tack one 2-1/2 inch nail through the jamb and the bottom shim pair.
58. At the **middle hinge** height, slide in a shim pair until it just touches both surfaces — no tighter.

    **Common Mistake:** driving the middle shims tight. The top and bottom set the line; a tight middle pair bows the jamb into a curve, and the door edge will rub it for the rest of the door's life. The middle pair only fills space.

59. Tack one nail through the middle pair.
60. Re-check both plumb directions and the drywall-flush check on the hinge jamb, and correct anything that moved.
61. Remove the shipping retainer (the plug, bracket, or small nails from step 17), keeping one hand on the door as you do.
62. Swing the door gently closed against the door stop (the thin strip of wood running around the inside of the frame that the door closes against).
63. Look at the gap between the top of the door and the head jamb. This gap is called the reveal (the visible gap between the door slab and its frame all the way around — it should end up even, about 1/8 inch, the thickness of two stacked quarters).
64. If the top reveal is wedge-shaped — more than 1/8 inch different from one end to the other — the frame is sitting tilted: pull the tack nails, lift the unit out, re-check the floor and your leg trim, correct it, and re-set from step 48. Annoying, but this is the moment it is still fixable.
65. On the latch side, with the door still closed, slide a shim pair in at knob height and adjust its depth until the reveal between the door edge and the latch jamb is an even 1/8 inch at that spot.
66. Tack one 2-1/2 inch nail through the latch jamb and that pair.
67. Slide in a shim pair about 6 inches down from the top of the latch jamb and set it so the reveal stays 1/8 inch along the top corner.
68. Tack one nail there.
69. Slide in a shim pair about 6 inches up from the floor on the latch jamb and set it for the same even reveal.
70. Tack one nail there.
71. Walk the whole reveal with the door closed — hinge side, top, latch side — and fine-tune shim depths until the gap reads an even 1/8 inch everywhere.

    **Common Mistake:** calling "close enough" on an uneven reveal. A door that shows a tight spot now will physically rub there by summer, because wood and doors move with humidity. Once the casing is on, fixing it means tearing the casing off. Even it out now — it is five minutes of nudging shims.

72. Swing test: open the door slowly and stop it at three positions — barely open, halfway, and wide open. The door must stay exactly where you leave it each time. If it drifts open or closed on its own, the hinge jamb is out of plumb in the in-and-out direction — go back to step 54, adjust, and test again before any final nailing.
73. Drive every tack nail fully home. If hand-nailing, stop hammering when the head is just above the wood and finish with the nail set to avoid half-moon hammer dents.
74. Drive one more 2-1/2 inch nail through each shim pack, so every pack holds two nails.

    **Common Mistake:** nailing the jamb where there is no shim behind it. A nail driven through the jamb into open air pulls the jamb toward the stud and bows it. Nails only ever go through shim packs.

75. Open the door and remove one screw from the **top hinge**, from the leaf on the jamb side — pick the screw hole nearest the door stop.
76. Drive the 3-inch screw into that hole until it is just snug, then stop. This screw reaches through the jamb into the jack stud and carries the door's weight for the long term, so the door does not sag over the years.

    **Common Mistake:** cranking the long screw tight. It is strong enough to drag the whole jamb sideways and pinch the top reveal shut. Snug it, look at the reveal at the top hinge, and back it off a quarter turn if the gap closed up.

77. Re-check the full reveal one more time after the screw goes in.
78. Score each protruding shim with the utility knife, right at the jamb edge, on both sides of the wall.
79. Snap each shim off at the score line by bending it, so every shim ends up hidden flush behind the jamb edges. Repeat for every shim pack on both sides of the wall.
80. Hold the two-foot level against the underside of the head jamb. If it is straight, leave it alone — the head jamb of a door this size needs no nails. If it bows down more than 1/16 inch in the middle, slide one shim pair in above it, snug only, and tack one nail through it.

> **Check Your Work — end of Phase 4.**
> **Look at:** the jamb edges against the drywall, the reveal, and the door's behavior.
> **Correct looks like:** the jamb edge is flush with the painted drywall along both sides and the top; the reveal is an even 1/8 inch all the way around the closed door; the door stays put at any open position; every shim pack has two fully driven nails; the long screw sits behind the top hinge; every shim is snapped off flush and invisible; nothing sticks out past the wall surface on the room side.

## Phase 5 — Test the Swing and Install the Door Knob

81. Open and close the door fully five times, watching and listening for any rubbing against the jambs and any scraping over the floor.
82. Close the door slowly and watch how the slab meets the door stop: it should touch the stop evenly from top to bottom on the latch side. If it touches at the bottom but stands off at the top (or the reverse), the frame has a slight twist — fix it now by easing the top or bottom latch-side shim pair slightly deeper or shallower until the door lands flat against the stop.
83. Slide the latch bolt into the round hole drilled in the door edge, oriented so its **sloped face points in the direction the door closes**. As the door shuts, the slope must ride over the strike plate and click in.

    **Common Mistake:** installing the latch backwards. Backwards, the flat face slams into the frame, the door bounces open, and it never clicks shut. Test the orientation before screwing anything: push the slope with your thumb in the closing direction — it should glide; the flat side should catch.

84. Screw the latch plate to the door edge with the screws from the knob kit, drilling small pilot holes (skinny starter holes that keep screws from splitting the wood) first if the screws turn hard.
85. Fit the two knob halves together through the latch body, following the kit's diagram, and drive their screws snug.
86. Turn each knob and watch the latch pull in and spring back smoothly.
87. Rub pencil lead on the front tip of the latch bolt, then close the door gently until the latch just touches the jamb — the pencil smudge left on the jamb marks the latch height.
88. Center the strike plate on that smudge, set back so the door face will rest snugly against the stop when latched, and trace the plate's outline and screw holes with the pencil.
89. Drill small pilot holes at the two screw marks.
90. If the plate should sit flush and the jamb has no factory recess for it: chisel out the traced outline about 1/16 inch deep so the plate beds in flush rather than sitting proud (sticking up above the surface).
91. Screw the strike plate on.
92. Close the door and check the latch: it must click in by itself and hold the door snug against the stop with no rattle when you push and pull on the closed door. If it rattles or will not click, loosen the plate, nudge it a hair toward or away from the stop, and re-tighten.

> **Check Your Work — end of Phase 5.**
> **Look at:** the swing, the latch, and the closed door.
> **Correct looks like:** the door swings its full travel silently and clears the floor; it stays wherever you stop it; it clicks shut on its own weight with a gentle push; the closed door does not rattle when pushed; both knobs turn freely.

## Phase 6 — Install the Door Casing

Casing is the flat trim that frames the doorway and hides the shim gap between the jamb and the wall. Two ideas rule this phase:

- **The casing reveal:** the casing never sits dead-even with the jamb's face edge. It is set back an even 1/4 inch from the inside face of the jamb, leaving a small, uniform step of jamb visible all the way around. That setback line is the reveal for casing, and marking it is the first task.
- **The miter joint:** at each top corner, the vertical piece and the horizontal piece meet at a 45 degree angle each (a corner joint where two trim pieces meet, each cut at forty-five degrees, forming a clean square corner).

The two upright casing pieces are called the legs, and the horizontal piece across the top is called the head.

**On this job, only the finished-room side gets cased today.** Steps 115 through 117 cover the workshop side.

93. Take the electrical box measurement from step 8 and confirm it is bigger than the casing width plus 1/2 inch. If it is not, stop and resolve the box with the person in charge before nailing any casing.
94. Set the combination square so its ruler extends exactly 1/4 inch.
95. Ride the square along the hinge jamb's inside face and make small pencil ticks 1/4 inch back from the edge — near the top, at the middle, and near the bottom.
96. Make the same three ticks along the latch jamb.
97. Make the same ticks along the head jamb.
98. At each top corner, extend the head line and the leg line with the pencil until they cross — each little cross is where the point of a miter joint will land.
99. Measure from the floor up to the cross mark on the hinge side. This is the length of the hinge-side leg measured along its **inside** edge (the edge nearest the door).
100. Cut the hinge-side leg: a square cut at the floor end, and a 45 degree miter at the top, with the **short side of the angle on the inside edge**, so the inside edge equals your measurement.

    **Common Mistake:** mitering the wrong direction. Before cutting, hold the piece up against the wall exactly where it will live and pencil the angle direction right on it. The short point always lands on the door side; the long point flares up and away. Trim stock cut backwards is scrap.

101. Stand the leg in place: inside edge on the reveal ticks, miter point touching the corner cross, bottom end resting on the floor.
102. Tack the leg with a few 1-1/4 inch brads through its inside edge into the jamb edge — enough to hold it, not the full pattern yet. If hand-nailing, leave the heads proud for now.
103. Measure floor-to-cross on the latch side separately, and cut the latch-side leg the same way but with the miter mirrored.

    **Common Mistake:** cutting the second leg to match the first without measuring. Floors are never perfectly even — half the time the two legs differ slightly, and a copied cut leaves one miter hanging open.

104. Tack the latch-side leg in place on its reveal ticks.
105. Measure between the two legs' miter short points — inside corner to inside corner. That is the head piece's length along its inside edge.
106. Cut the head with a 45 degree miter at each end, angles facing opposite ways, inside edge equal to your measurement — and cut it a hair long on purpose (about 1/32 inch), then shave it down in tiny re-cuts until it just fits. Sneaking up on the fit beats cutting it short in one go.
107. Dry-fit the head (set it in place with no glue or nails) and inspect both corners: the miter faces should meet tight along their whole line.
108. If a corner shows a gap, adjust — shave the head's angle a hair, or ease a leg's tack nails and shift it a fraction — until both corners close.
109. Brush a thin coat of wood glue on the miter faces of the head and legs.
110. Set the head on its reveal ticks so both corners close tight.
111. Nail the head: 1-1/4 inch brads through its inside edge into the head jamb, and 2 inch nails through its outside edge into the header framing above.
112. Lock each miter: drive one 1-1/4 inch brad through the edge of the casing sideways across the joint, pinning the two pieces to each other while the glue dries. If hand-nailing, drill a pilot hole first — a bare nail this close to the end will split the trim.
113. Finish nailing both legs in the full pattern: 1-1/4 inch brads through the inside edge into the jamb about every 12 inches, and 2 inch nails through the outside edge into the jack stud about every 16 inches.

    **Common Mistake:** using the long 2 inch nails along the inside edge. The inside edge sits over the jamb and the door gap — a long nail there can shoot through the jamb, hit the door slab, or block the door from closing. Long nails belong only on the outside edge, where solid framing sits behind the wall.

114. Wipe off every bit of squeezed-out glue with the damp rag now, while it is wet — dried glue smears reject paint and show forever.
115. Now the workshop side. Hold the four-foot level flat across the bare stud faces beside the jamb and measure how far the jamb edge sticks out past the studs. Expect about 1/2 inch — that is the extra jamb depth from step 43 doing its job of waiting for future drywall.
116. Because this side has no wall surface yet, **do not install casing on the workshop side today.** Casing needs a flat, finished wall to land on. When drywall (or another finished wall surface) goes up on the workshop side and sits flush with the jamb edge, come back and repeat steps 94 through 114 on that side, exactly the same way.

    **Common Mistake:** casing over the bare studs "just for now." The casing would only touch wood at each stud, with hollow gaps everywhere between, and it would all have to be pried off — bending the trim and chewing up the new jamb — the day drywall arrives. Waiting costs nothing; ripping it off later costs trim and patience.

117. Write a note for the person in charge: workshop-side casing is on hold until that side of the wall is finished, and the casing stock for it is purchased and stored.

> **Check Your Work — end of Phase 6.**
> **Look at:** the reveal line, the corners, and the door's operation.
> **Correct looks like:** an even 1/4 inch band of jamb face shows inside the casing all the way around, never wandering; both miter corners are closed tight with no dark gap line; the casing lies flat against both the wall and the jamb edge; no nail sticks up proud; the door still swings and latches exactly as it did before casing went on; the electrical box is fully clear of the trim and still uncovered by it.

## Phase 7 — Install the Baseboard Up to the Casing

Order of operations, and why: **casing first, then baseboard.** The casing runs all the way to the floor, and the baseboard runs along the wall and dies into the casing's side edge. This works because casing stock is thicker than baseboard stock, so the baseboard end tucks against it with a clean shadow line. If baseboard went in first, the casing would have to be notched around it — an ugly, weak joint.

One floor rule before nailing: this room's floor is a floating floor. The baseboard gets nailed **to the wall only — never down into the floor** — and it should rest lightly on the planks, not press them down, so the floor stays free to move underneath.

118. Hold a scrap of baseboard against the side edge of an installed casing leg and confirm the casing stands prouder (thicker off the wall) than the baseboard. It normally does. If the baseboard is the thicker one, stop and ask the person in charge how they want the joint handled before cutting anything.
119. Run the stud finder along the wall between the casing and the room corner, and mark each stud location lightly in pencil about 4 inches above the floor, where the mark will stay visible above the installed baseboard.
120. Measure along the floor from the casing's side edge to the room corner.
121. Cut a baseboard piece square (a straight 90 degree cut) at the end that meets the casing.
122. Cut the other end to suit the corner it meets: square if it dies into the corner, or at 45 degrees if the plan is to miter it to the next wall's piece. For inside corners, mitering both meeting pieces at 45 degrees is the simplest beginner method; ask the person in charge if they prefer coped joints (a method where one piece is cut to wrap the other's profile) — coping is better but is a skill for another day.
123. Dry-fit the piece: the square end should sit tight against the casing with no gap, and the piece should lie flat along the wall. Shave the corner end in small re-cuts if it is long.
124. Nail the baseboard with 2 inch finish nails: at every stud mark, drive one nail near the top of the board into the stud, and one nail about 1 inch up from the floor into the bottom plate (the horizontal framing board that runs along the floor at the base of every wall).

    **Common Mistake:** angling nails downward into the floor, or nailing below the bottom plate into nothing. A nail into the floating floor pins the planks, and a pinned floating floor can buckle into a hump when seasons change. Keep every nail horizontal and in the wall framing.

125. As you nail, let the baseboard rest on the planks with only its own light contact — do not push it down hard onto the floor.
126. Repeat steps 119 through 125 for the wall on the other side of the doorway, measuring that wall on its own.
127. If hand-nailing, set every baseboard nail head just below the surface with the nail set.

> **Check Your Work — end of Phase 7.**
> **Look at:** the joint at the casing, the top edge line, and the floor.
> **Correct looks like:** the baseboard end meets the casing side with a tight seam; the top edge runs along the wall with no gaps bigger than about 1/16 inch (small caulkable gaps are normal on wavy drywall); every nail went into framing and none into the floor; the board touches the floor gently without visibly bowing it down; corner joints are closed.

## Phase 8 — Fill Nail Holes, Caulk the Gaps, and Touch Up Paint

What gets which product — this is the part beginners mix up:

- **Wood filler** goes in nail holes and miter seams — anything that needs to dry hard and sand flat and crisp.
- **Caulk** goes in the long seams where trim meets wall — anything that needs to flex. Caulk cannot be sanded.

128. Vacuum the trim and wipe it down with the damp rag so filler and caulk stick to wood, not dust.
129. Run your fingertips over every piece of trim and sink any nail head you can feel with the nail set, about 1/16 inch below the surface.
130. Press wood filler into every nail hole with the putty knife or a fingertip, leaving each hole slightly overfilled.
131. Press filler into the miter seam lines at the casing corners and into any dents or dings.

    **Common Mistake:** caulking the nail holes because the caulk gun is already in hand. Caulk shrinks back into the hole as it dries and flashes shiny through paint — every filled hole shows as a dimple. Holes get filler; only long wall seams get caulk.

132. Let the filler dry for the time printed on its tube — usually 30 to 60 minutes.
133. Refill any hole that dried sunken, and let it dry again.
134. Sand every filled spot flush with the fine sanding sponge, using light pressure.
135. Wipe the sanding dust off with the damp rag and let the trim dry.
136. Cut the caulk tube tip at a slight angle, making a small opening about 1/8 inch across, and pierce the inner seal, so the gun lays a thin bead (a continuous line of caulk).
137. Run a bead along the seam where the casing's outer edge meets the wall — both legs and the head.
138. Run a hairline bead along the seam where the casing's inner edge meets the jamb, anywhere a gap shows.
139. Run a bead along the baseboard's top edge where it meets the wall.
140. Run a bead down the vertical joint where the baseboard end meets the casing.
141. Leave these places **uncaulked**: the gap between the baseboard and the floor (the floating floor must stay free, and caulk there collects grime), the reveal gap around the door slab (caulk there glues the door shut), and the hinges and latch hardware.
142. Smooth each fresh bead right after laying it, with a damp fingertip or the damp sponge, in one steady wiping pass. Work one seam at a time — caulk skins over in minutes.
143. Let the caulk dry until it is paint-ready, per its tube — usually 30 to 60 minutes.
144. Mask for paint: painter's tape over the hinges, around the knob, and along the floor planks against the baseboard.
145. Brush the first thin coat of trim paint on everything that is bare or filled: the casing, the baseboard, the jamb faces and door stop, and the door slab itself — the factory white on all of it is only primer (a base coat meant to be painted over), not a finish. On the six-panel slab, paint the recessed panels first, then the flat areas, keeping the coat thin.
146. Leave the door standing open while paint dries, and keep it open until the paint is fully dry.

    **Common Mistake:** closing a freshly painted door "just to check the fit." The paint glues the slab to the stop, and opening it later peels a strip of finish off both surfaces. The door stays open until everything is hard-dry — overnight is safest.

147. Let the first coat dry for the time printed on the can — usually 2 to 4 hours.
148. Brush on a second thin coat and let it dry the same way.
149. Touch up the wall paint where the work scuffed it — gray on the gray walls, navy on the navy wall — with the small brush.
150. Pull the painter's tape off slowly at a low angle once the paint is dry to the touch.
151. Have the person in charge or an electrician install the blank cover plate (or the planned switch) on the open electrical box, and only then turn the breaker back on. An open box with exposed wires means the job is not finished.
152. Clean up: fold the drop cloth outward so debris stays inside it, vacuum the floor, move the shelf and bin back, and return the tools.

> **Check Your Work — end of Phase 8 (final).**
> **Look at:** the trim under raking light (light shining along the surface), the door's operation, and the electrical box.
> **Correct looks like:** no nail dimples or filler humps visible or felt; caulk lines are thin, smooth, and unbroken; paint coverage is even with no drips or brush ropes; the door still swings freely, stays put, and latches with a click; the floor has no paint spots and the baseboard does not pin it down; the electrical box has a cover plate on it and power is back on.

## Quick Reference Checklist

Screenshot this page. Work top to bottom.

**Safety and setup**
- [ ] Breaker off, every wire near the opening tested dead with the tester
- [ ] Opening-to-electrical-box distance measured and written down (must be over 4 inches)
- [ ] Floor covered; saw set up in the workshop, never over the new floor

**Unpack and inspect**
- [ ] Straps cut away from body; cardboard saved for floor protection
- [ ] Shipping retainer left in until the frame is in the opening
- [ ] No dents or splits; hinge screws snug
- [ ] Written down: unit width, unit height, jamb depth
- [ ] Door will swing into the finished room; knob side checked against the switch box

**Rough opening**
- [ ] Opening is 1/2 inch to 1-1/4 inches wider than the unit, and taller by at least 3/8 inch
- [ ] Floor checked with the level — high side and amount written down
- [ ] Both sides checked for plumb, both directions; diagonals within 1/4 inch
- [ ] Rule remembered: jamb sits flush with the painted room side; extra depth hangs into the workshop

**Set the frame**
- [ ] Leg on the floor's high side trimmed by the floor error, if over 1/8 inch
- [ ] Shims in opposing pairs only; hinge side first — top hinge, bottom hinge, then middle just touching
- [ ] Hinge jamb plumb both directions; jamb flush with drywall
- [ ] Reveal even at 1/8 inch all around, latch side shimmed to match
- [ ] Door stays put barely open, halfway, and wide open — before final nailing
- [ ] Two nails per shim pack, nowhere else; 3 inch screw snug in the top hinge
- [ ] Shims scored and snapped flush

**Swing and latch**
- [ ] Latch slope faces the closing direction; latch clicks and holds with no rattle

**Casing (finished room side only, today)**
- [ ] Reveal ticks marked 1/4 inch back on both jambs and the head
- [ ] Legs measured separately, floor to corner cross, short point on the door side
- [ ] Head cut long, shaved to fit; miters glued, nailed, and pinned across the corner
- [ ] Brads only into the jamb edge; 2 inch nails only into the framing edge
- [ ] Workshop side: no casing until that wall has drywall — note left for the person in charge

**Baseboard**
- [ ] Casing installed first; baseboard cut square where it meets the casing
- [ ] Studs marked; nails into studs and bottom plate only — never into the floor
- [ ] Board rests lightly on the floating floor

**Finish**
- [ ] Filler in every nail hole and miter seam, sanded flush — caulk never used in holes
- [ ] Caulk on wall seams only — never on the floor gap or around the door slab
- [ ] Two thin coats of trim paint; door stays open until paint is hard-dry
- [ ] Wall colors touched up; tape pulled at a low angle
- [ ] Cover plate on the electrical box, then power back on

**The final test:** the door swings silently, stays wherever you leave it, clicks shut with a gentle push, shows an even gap all around, and the trim shows one straight 1/4 inch reveal line with tight corners. That is what done looks like.
